# Chapter 1  
## E City

E City is both a city and an independent nation.

It lies on a tiny planet in a remote star system, far from the other descendants of Earth. The planet’s orbit and its axis of rotation lie on the same plane, like a perfectly balanced spinning top in eternal motion. There are no seasons here. Day and night are equal in length. Under the quiet rule of advanced technology, the climate remains perpetually gentle—no tropical cyclones, no polar high-pressure systems, no extremes of heat or cold.

When the city was first built, its location, design, and construction represented the very pinnacle of human achievement: the finest technology, the purest ideals, and the most compassionate design humanity could offer.

It had been created for a particular group of people.

Centuries earlier, a genetic engineering project had produced a large number of children who lost their sight during development. The city was built for them.

Yet E City is more widely known by another name.

**Blind City.**

To outsiders, it is often imagined as a city inhabited entirely by blind people. That assumption is not entirely accurate.

Among the city’s roughly one hundred thousand residents, about one thousand possess some degree of vision. Only a hundred of them have completely normal sight. The remaining nine hundred live with different forms of visual impairment—monocular blindness, low vision, total color blindness.

The other ninety-nine percent of the population are completely blind.

Even within that majority there are differences. Roughly one tenth lost their sight later in life through accidents or illness and came here seeking refuge. The remaining nine tenths are descendants of the prodigies born during the famous genetic experiment three centuries ago. They are considered the city’s original inhabitants.

For complicated historical reasons, many residents do not accept the name *Blind City*. Few people enjoy being constantly reminded of what they lack.

Still, the name is short, easy to say, and it slips out from time to time.

Out of respect, whenever residents of the city are present, this book will try to call it by its proper name—**E City**. If the other name appears by accident, the author offers sincere apologies. Fighting prejudice is rarely simple, and the author is far from a model citizen.

In its early years, the city was founded as a refuge for people who could not easily survive in the mainstream societies of the time—societies dominated by those with normal vision. The spirit of protection, care, and inclusion drew not only its future residents but also many volunteers who helped construct the city. Many of those volunteers could see perfectly well.

History, however, has its turning points.

After several turbulent periods and growing security concerns, the city changed its policies. Over the past fifty years, Blind City has no longer been fully open to travelers. People from the outside world cannot freely enter or leave.

Instead, a transitional zone was established outside the city walls.

Visitors and residency applicants stay there first. Anyone wishing to become a citizen must live in this zone for a period of time, learning how life in the city works, understanding the responsibilities of its people, and passing a series of tests before being allowed to move into the main city.

At this moment, a group of four travelers is heading toward that place.

The veiled woman is **S**. Even with a white veil covering her face, every movement carries the quiet aura of extraordinary beauty. Blind City is her destination.

The pilot of the small spacecraft is **K**, S’s longtime friend. He has accompanied her all the way here.

The short-haired young woman is **L**, a girl suffering from amnesia. Several weeks earlier, S happened to save her life. So far, the only thing she remembers about herself is her name.

The curly-haired man lighting a cigarette is **J**, a magician. One week ago, in a small desert town, he and the other three shared a brief but surprisingly eventful journey together.

Night slowly falls.

They say that the nights in Blind City are very dark.

And that the stars are astonishingly bright.
