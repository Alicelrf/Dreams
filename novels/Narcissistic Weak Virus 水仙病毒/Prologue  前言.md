这是故事的开头也是故事的结尾。

我已经很久都没有遇到J了。偶尔我会梦见P，以及我自己过去某个时期的心情，但是很快我就能分辨这只是记忆里的P。无论多么的神似，即使梦里也有跌宕起伏的心情，嬉笑怒骂的日常，但醒来我总是知道他俩谁是谁。

我一度以为J只是一个美化了的P，是少年时期的我无法处理P带来的复杂的创伤，又渴望留住与那人的默契，从而在脑海里创造出的形象。但J他从来没有提起过任何现实里P与我的过去，更没有为P的所作所为道歉过（那倒也是他们根本不是同一个灵魂）。所以我逐渐意识到，J可能只是他自己，或者他可能是我的一部分。J他真的如P在很多年前承诺的那样在我身边陪我长大，即使P早已在十多年前就断了联系，而且就在我在现实里遇到真正的爱情的时候，J消失了。

我最后一次见到他的时候，我很困惑于他的异常举动，但我以为他只是发神经，毕竟他是个常常令人无语的无赖魔术师。说是异常，他也没有什么常态可言。

很久不见他之后，我开始努力回忆最后的那次相遇。那是第一次我见他流泪狂笑地像个小丑。我不理解。我无法接受那就是他给我的告别。在那最后一面之后的一两年，我试过回忆他的各种事迹，我们的共同经历，甚至翻出高中的日记想要用真实的与P的回忆激他出来。全都没有用，可能偶尔有几次梦见了P。

想到J这个人物除了我世界上没有人认识他，我们的故事我从未向人提起。与那个处处留情倒出造业的P不同，以后我死去，J就会如同从来不曾存在过一样。在放弃了寻找他之后，我开始记录收集起了J的故事。这事我以前也不是没想过要做，但是这个人的嘲讽技能点满，让人在他面前完全不能掉以轻心。要是让他知道了，我能立刻想到他的嘴脸，现在倒是没有他打扰我了，可我每每带着再也见不到这人伤感的心情提笔，又总是语调不对，毕竟J做的大部分事情都是无聊或搞笑的。

时过境迁，我已经接受了J彻底消失在我的神经信号里永不再出现。再说我生活也很忙，工作，搬家，异地恋，和不退押金的房东打官司。一个只存在于我脑海里的始于创伤基于记忆的一个人物，听起来都感觉有点矫情，写了也不好意思与人分享。那写与不写不都是要被人遗忘的吗？再说了，就算我死去了，可能些微能在10年以内，在世界上留下一丁点痕迹，然后又怎么样。谁的存在不都是要被时间抹去的吗？如此想着，我也不再执着于写关于J的故事，这期间倒是写了七七八八别的短篇故事。

近期的某一天，在我独自游历了一番的惊险刺激的梦之后，疲惫且混乱地躺着等待闹钟响起。这些梦我还创作着，但是少了那彩蛋一样的J。恍惚中我又迷迷糊糊地睡去。我看到了一段视频。


我通过乱糟糟地卷发在光线昏暗的视频里辨认出J胡子拉渣的脸还有不知多久没睡的眼睛。 他在酒吧的角落的一个小方桌打牌，时不时以椅子的后两条腿为支点后仰，侧对着镜头的是P，一样的脸收拾得干净很多。P问J，“你真的再不回她了吗？” 

J 只专注出牌，出完了牌就盯着手里的牌，“不需要。”

“你知道她这段时间都在找你吗？”

“上周不是让你假装我回去吗？”

“她又不想见我。你不会真觉得她分辨不出来我俩吧。”P几不可闻地叹了口气，不改那半永久一般略微嘲讽略的上扬嘴角，出了一对“J”。

“那你这演技太差了。再说她都不知道咱俩有联系吧。”J甩下两张牌，最后一张覆盖在桌上。“一对A，单爆。”

然后在J刚抬头偶然看向镜头的时候，视频晃动着结束了。


闹铃响起。
我是跟着我的怒气一起从床上升起的。难以置信。

刚醒来时分的惊讶与恼怒很快被一阵风吹散了，一阵清泠泠的名叫释怀的风。终于我也明白了，他是打定主意不再见我了。那么这之后见与不见，都不再重要了。那么过去的故事，也终于只属于我了。
# Prologue  
## Where the Story Begins, and Ends

This is where the story begins.

And also where it ends.

I have not seen J for a very long time.

Sometimes I still dream of P. In those dreams I encounter fragments of who I used to be—the moods, the tempers, the strange intensity of those years. But it never takes long for me to realize that the person in the dream is only the **P preserved in memory**.

No matter how convincing the resemblance may be—no matter how turbulent the emotions, how vivid the laughter, the quarrels, the ordinary rhythms of life—when I wake up I always know which one of them he is.

For a while, I believed that J was simply a softened version of P.

Perhaps the younger version of myself, unable to process the complicated wounds P had left behind yet unwilling to abandon the silent understanding we once shared, had invented J as a compromise—a figure my mind created so that something could remain.

But J never spoke of P.

He never mentioned the past that P and I had lived through.  
He never apologized for what P had done.

Which, in retrospect, makes perfect sense.

They were never the same soul.

Slowly I began to suspect that J might simply be himself.

Or perhaps he was something stranger—  
a fragment of me that learned how to stand on its own.

J stayed with me as I grew up. In a way, he fulfilled the promise P once made many years ago: that he would remain beside me while I learned how to become an adult.

P himself vanished from my life more than a decade ago.

J remained.

Until the moment I finally encountered real love in the world.

That was when he disappeared.

---

The last time I saw him, I did not understand what was happening.

His behavior struck me as strange, but I dismissed it. J was, after all, a magician—an insolent one at that—and he had never behaved in a way that could reasonably be called normal.

Calling that moment unusual is perhaps unfair.

J had no normal state to begin with.

Only later, long after he vanished, did I begin trying to reconstruct our final meeting.

It was the first time I had ever seen him laugh and cry at the same time.

Not quietly.

Not discreetly.

But wildly—like a clown on a stage no one else could see.

I did not understand it then.

And I could not accept that this had been his farewell.

For a year or two after that moment, I tried everything I could think of to bring him back.

I replayed memories of our shared adventures. I searched through fragments of the past. I even dug out my high school diaries, hoping that recalling the real memories I had with P might somehow provoke J into returning.

None of it worked.

Occasionally I dreamed of P.

Never J.

---

Then one day I realized something unsettling.

No one in the world knows J exists.

I have never told anyone about him.

Unlike P—who left traces of himself everywhere, who scattered consequences through the lives of others—J exists nowhere outside my mind.

When I die, he will disappear as if he had never existed.

That was when I decided to begin writing down his stories.

It was not the first time the thought had occurred to me. But J possessed a particular gift for mockery. One could never lower one’s guard around him. If he had ever discovered what I was doing, I can already imagine the expression he would have worn.

Now he is no longer here to interrupt me.

And yet writing about him remains strangely difficult.

Perhaps because most of the things J ever did were either ridiculous, absurd, or unintentionally funny.

It is difficult to write elegies for a man who behaved like that.

---

Life, meanwhile, continued.

Work. Moving apartments. Long-distance relationships. Lawsuits with landlords who refuse to return deposits.

A character born from trauma and memory who exists only inside my mind—it already sounds embarrassingly sentimental when written down. Even if I finished the story, who would I share it with?

Whether I write it or not, everything will eventually be forgotten.

Even if I die, perhaps the faintest trace of my existence might linger somewhere for ten years.

And then what?

Time erases everyone.

So I stopped trying to write J’s story.

Instead, I wrote other short stories. Many of them.

---

Until one recent morning.

After a long night of vivid dreams, I lay exhausted in bed waiting for the alarm to ring. I still record these dreams sometimes. They are elaborate, strange, occasionally beautiful.

But they no longer contain the small hidden surprise J used to be.

Half-awake, I drifted back into sleep.

And then I saw a video.

The lighting was dim, but I recognized him immediately: the unruly curls, the unshaven face, the sleepless eyes.

J was sitting in the corner of a bar at a small square table, playing cards.

Every so often he leaned back in his chair, balancing on the two back legs.

Across from him, half-turned toward the camera, sat P.

The same face.

But cleaner. Sharper. More composed.

P asked him quietly,

“Are you really never going back to her?”

J kept his eyes on his cards.

“No need.”

“You know she’s been looking for you.”

“Didn’t I ask you to pretend to be me last week?”

“She didn’t want to see me. You don’t seriously think she can’t tell the difference between us.”

P sighed softly, though the faint curve of mockery at the corner of his mouth never changed.

He laid down a pair of jacks.

“Then your acting must be terrible,” J said.

He dropped two cards onto the table and covered them with the last one.

“A pair of aces.”

“One-shot win.”

For a moment he looked up.

Toward the camera.

The video ended.

---

Then the alarm rang.

I sat up in bed with a surge of anger so sudden it almost felt absurd.

But the anger dissolved quickly, scattered by something colder and clearer.

Relief.

At last I understood.

He had decided not to see me again.

And if that was the case, whether we ever met again no longer mattered.

The past now belonged only to me.
