Last night before going to bed, I finished reading At Day’s End by Jenny, a writer born in East Germany.

Afterward, I dreamed that I was a judge tasked with awarding the Socialist Model Worker Prize to fictional characters from literature. The organizers told me, “You are fictional as well, so you can award it to yourself.” I replied, “That wouldn’t be right. I’m far too lazy—I could never live up to it.”

To ensure fairness, the candidates were assigned a team task, though they were not told it was for the award: they were to dive underwater and salvage antique furniture, restore and refurbish it, and then sell it. I had to participate in every stage—both lending a hand and observing their diligence and attitude toward labor.

The first stage was salvage, led by Lin Daiyu. It seemed that this former frail young aristocrat had been thoroughly nourished and strengthened by socialist modern society. Together we descended with scuba gear. She dove into the wreck alone while I supported from nearby. I watched as she effortlessly flung a chair-shaped object from five meters away—such strength, against the weight of water! Clearly Lin Daiyu the diver had long been hardened by labor. When I caught the oil-paper-wrapped piece of furniture, I could still feel its momentum as I swam it back to the boat. In only a few days, we had brought up a full dining set.

At the second candidate’s workshop, Catherine from Wuthering Heights had already completed the restoration of the first chair, a Rococo-style dining chair. She peeled away the layers of protective paper, removed stains, and replaced broken wood. I wanted to interview her, but the severity of her gaze silenced me; I stood watching, not daring to disturb her. Was this truly the same Catherine who once ran wild across the moors, unable to sit still? When she finished the second chair, her old, carefree self returned. “I’m parched—fetch me some water,” she cried, wiping her brow with her filthy work clothes until her face was streaked and smudged.

The third candidate was Lady Tomoyo of the sales team. Ah, Tomoyo—forever graceful, elegant, gentle, and patient. She is the very image of my ideal. On a hot June day, dressed in a business suit and high heels, she could still maintain her poise and smile with quiet dignity as she greeted the next potential buyer.

I, meanwhile, was of little use. I grew anxious about whom the prize should go to. Perceptive Tomoyo noticed my unease and came to console me. When she heard about the prize behind the project, she insisted that it should go to someone who had labored harder; she herself had only spoken with customers in the city, and it had been no great hardship.

The other two were equally modest, which left me in a bind. I even considered whether I had worked hard enough myself, and whether I ought to award it to myself and divide the prize equally among us. Perhaps this, I thought, is how the corruption of communism begins.

By the time the alarm woke me, I still had not decided to whom the prize should go.
