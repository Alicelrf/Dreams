Dream on 2025-4-8

Perhaps because I was about to have dinner with someone who graduated from the same high school as me, memories from more than ten years ago quietly stretched out their gloomy tentacles from the dim seabed, swaying slowly to remind me that they had never truly disappeared.

In the dream, I was working on a math mock exam. The second-to-last big problem was analytic geometry—the kind I hated the most: ellipses, hyperbolas, and parabolas. I had already calculated it three times and gotten three different answers. I looked at the last big problem. It was about sequences—slightly less annoying, but still unlikely that I could solve it correctly.

My head was spinning from all the calculations. I looked out the window. A group of workers were building a white building outside. From the podium came a meaningful cough, as if reminding me to focus on the exam and stop looking around.

I looked toward the podium. The head of the dean of discipline—bald in the typical “Mediterranean” style—should have been there supervising the exam today. But instead, there was an owl crouching there, swiveling its head dramatically back and forth. Its wide eyes were extremely sharp.

I stared at the owl.

The owl stared back at me.

I felt that this owl looked very familiar. It knew me.

“Ten minutes left,” came the dean’s voice from the back of the classroom.

I hurriedly wrote down the analytic geometry answer from my final calculation. For the sequence problem I only finished the first sub-question before handing in the paper. We had a ten-minute break, and then the Chinese exam would begin.

I got up to stretch my legs, and suddenly realized that the dean’s voice was coming from the bulging mouth of a bright green frog with a white belly.

Carefully, I asked my deskmate,

“Do you see that frog?”

“What frog? Did you fry your brain with a fever?”

My deskmate was reviewing classical Chinese texts.

“That one over there. It’s huge. Smooth skin.”

I pointed toward the back of the classroom. A big frog was holding a stack of exam papers with its green five-fingered claws, counting them while its long pink tongue flicked out to lick them one by one.

The frog heard me talking about it and saw that I was pointing at it. Enraged and embarrassed, it hopped over and called me out of the classroom. It was truly furious—its belly puffed out enormously.

My mind was already feeling hazy. Seeing how absent-minded I looked, it tapped me on the head with a rolled-up exam paper. Only then did I realize that standing in front of me was actually the dean of discipline.

The dean, still angry, continued:

“Do you even know what grade you’re in now? There’s no time left for you to fool around!”

He launched into a long lecture—about having a sense of urgency, about intense competition—phrases I had heard countless times before. But my thoughts drifted away at one of his rhetorical questions.

Right… what grade am I in now?

In my memory, first year of high school meant doing one or two exam papers a day.

Second year meant three or four.

Third year meant five a day.

Today we had the math exam, then Chinese, and English in the afternoon.

So I must be either in second year or third year.

But how long until the college entrance exam?

The class bell rang. The dean finally let me back into the classroom to start the next subject: Chinese.

When the exam paper was handed out, I glanced first at the essay prompt as usual. It was a material-based essay titled “Fantasy.”

Then I started answering the earlier questions, but in the background my mind kept thinking about how to write the essay.

Fantasy…

Was that frog my fantasy?

Where did the frog go just now?

It still had my math paper.

Gradually my thoughts drifted farther away. I stared blankly out the window again at the construction workers in the distance.

What were they building?

I wanted to go outside and move around too.

How much longer would this life of sitting in a classroom for more than ten hours a day last?

What grade am I in now?

Why am I even writing all these exam papers?

At that moment, a tiny frog appeared on the blank page of my essay. It was only about the size of my palm, but it looked exactly like the frog that had been the dean earlier.

I poked its soft belly with my finger.

It clutched its stomach and hopped away.

“Hey! Don’t poke me. Just write your essay properly.”

“Haha, I don’t want to write anymore. Why am I even writing these exam papers? It’s kind of stupid.”

“If you don’t write the exams, how will you get into a good university?”

“Why do I need to go to a good university?”

“If you don’t go to university, then all these exam papers you wrote would be wasted! If you don’t go to university, how will you get a job? And without a job, how will you earn money to send your own kids to university?”

The frog spoke with great confidence.

“So… what is a university?”

I was feeling extremely tired.

“Look outside at that building under construction. They’re building a university. You students write exam papers, your parents earn money, and then those exam papers and money are stacked layer by layer like bricks—and that’s how a mental hospital gets built.”

“What? Did you just say a mental hospital?”

I suddenly jolted awake.

“No no no—you heard wrong. I said university.”

The frog shrank back nervously.

“I definitely didn’t hear wrong. You said that we write exam papers and our parents pay money just to send us into a mental hospital.”

It felt like a sudden moment of enlightenment. I loudly questioned the frog.

“Don’t overthink it! Why would anyone send you to a mental hospital? You’re just tired.”

The frog raised one hand to cover its face, but I couldn’t shake the feeling that its big eyes looked shifty.

“Why send us to a mental hospital? Isn’t it obvious? So we can keep writing exam papers!”

I felt as if I had discovered something extremely important and jumped to my feet. But my classmates were completely unaffected—they just kept quietly bending over their desks, filling in the little squares of their answer sheets.

The frog suddenly produced several small red pills from somewhere and eagerly pushed them toward me.

“Come, come, sit down. Don’t scare your classmates. You’ve just been using your brain too much. Here—have some energy candy and hurry up and finish your essay.”

To hell with your energy candy.

To hell with your exam papers.

To hell with your university.

I know this is all a trap—to turn us into mental patients and send us into that mental hospital that’s about to be completed!

Angrily, I grabbed the frog and shook it upside down. Several of those red pills fell out of its mouth. I slapped its backside and even more poured out with a clatter.

“I’m going to see what your real identity is!”

Just then I was surrounded. All around me were shouting voices I couldn’t understand. I tried desperately to break free. As I struggled, I shouted for my deskmate—my friend—to save me, telling them to stop writing.

But through the gaps I saw that my classmates were actually just dolls placed on the floor to keep me company while studying.
And finally I remembered.
I was already in the mental hospital.
