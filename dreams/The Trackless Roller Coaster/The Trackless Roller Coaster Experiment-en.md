Last night I dreamed that I was participating in a competition to design and invent a **trackless roller coaster**.

Because I was about to miss the deadline but still insisted on finishing my design, I forced a scientist friend of mine—basically a mad scientist—to **clone me** and leave the clone in the real world to present the results on the morning of the exhibition. Meanwhile, I myself went back into the **past timeline** to redo the experiment.

I repeated this **three times**.

The first two times, I had to return to the real world in frustration. Each time I came back, I would see my mad scientist friend **tricking my clone into entering sleep mode**, and then freezing her.

I felt terribly guilty watching it happen. I was also worried that **maybe I was a clone too**, and that one day I would end up being frozen like that.

So I asked him about it.

He shrugged indifferently and said,  
“You’re the real one. Otherwise would I let you see this?”

He added that they weren’t even full clones—just **dummy bodies that looked like me**. They didn’t even have complete memories. He had been telling the staff that **I had been in a serious car accident yesterday and that my helmet had been damaged and was under repair**.

_(The helmet is issued during the competition. It cannot be removed and serves as the only identity verification for participants. The idea is that if plagiarism disputes occur in the future, the organizers can review the entire thought process recorded in the helmet to prove originality. In reality, because it involves so much personal data, it is rarely used unless the plagiarism case is extremely serious. Most of the time it simply functions as the competitor’s ID. Of course, me going back to the past like this is already cheating—but the helmet can’t be taken off, so it time-travels with me.)_

But eventually my friend warned me:

“The final test is in one hour. You can only go back **one more time**. I can’t keep watching your clones forever—I have other things to do today. If you’re not back at the venue in an hour, you’ll lose your qualification to compete.”

So I went back **one last time**.

And finally, in the past timeline, I designed a long flying vehicle that twisted and coiled through the air like a **loach—no, scratch that—like a snake—no, scratch that—like a dragon**.

It moved rapidly through the air in spirals and curves.

This meant the roller coaster’s path could be **modified dynamically**, and people sitting in different positions would have **completely different experiences**.

At last I returned to the real world in time for the presentation.

In the dream I somehow managed to rush back during the **final ten minutes before the exhibition**. I explained to the staff that the reason my helmet was missing was because **I had gotten drunk celebrating the completion of the design the night before**, and my friend, unable to find me, had come up with this ridiculous story about a car accident in order to help me keep my qualification.

But once my **dragon roller coaster** appeared, it instantly crushed all the other designs.

There was something called a **capsule roller coaster**, for example. It still had intermediate stations—basically just a small sphere flying through the air and being accelerated at several points.

Then there was… well… a rather old-fashioned roller coaster that just **swung around hanging from a cable**.

None of them were truly trackless.

The most outrageous design came from someone clearly benefiting from connections. What he presented wasn’t even a trackless roller coaster at all—it was basically **a giant set of tubes like the ones in old water parks**.

The other contestants were furious.

So my friend secretly released **two flying robotic dogs** right before that guy’s presentation. They sabotaged the giant tube structure, and the guy’s already-questionable project was forced to sit quietly on the sidelines where it belonged.

Seeing him raging in anger made the other contestants exchange amused glances with each other.

After presenting my design, I didn’t stay for the award ceremony. Instead, my friend and I **rode the roller coaster and flew away**.

After all, I had cheated. It didn’t feel right to accept the prize.

Later I heard that **first place was awarded to the mid-boost capsule roller coaster**.
