I had another dream last night. I wrote it down in a hurry when I woke up.

In the dream, it was some kind of future. JC had moved to a city in the American Midwest where a massive model-training center was located — the largest AI research hub in the United States. I had just arrived in that city, and everything felt new to me. JC said that since he moved there he rarely went out to eat; he usually ate inside the company campus. The campus security was strict, and going in and out was a hassle. Since he had already come out to pick me up, we decided to wander around the nearby downtown area instead.

We went to an Italian restaurant. The reviews suggested it was leaning toward fine dining. We sat outside on the patio. Even though it was early autumn, the Midwestern sunlight was still harsh and glaring. After we ordered, while waiting for the food, I suddenly noticed something strange.

On the ring finger of my left hand were two intersecting circles — an intricate ring tattoo.

I began studying it carefully. Why was there a tattoo here? Why a ring? Had I lost my memory?

I quietly glanced at JC’s hand. He was wearing a real wedding ring on his left ring finger.

I asked him, “Why do you have a real ring while mine is a tattoo?”

JC laughed. “Oh, you forgot again. You already lost two of them. When you lost the second one, you got so angry that you went and tattooed both rings onto your finger.”

That explanation surprised me, yet it didn’t feel completely impossible. I actually liked the tattoos, so I accepted it happily.

Then I noticed something else. On the back of my right hand, near where the veins usually run, there were several faint lines that looked like small rivers — almost like veins at first glance, but on closer look more like tattooed patterns. I examined them with curiosity.

Alright, I thought, maybe I have intermittent memory loss. If the wedding ring is tattooed, then I must already have a habit of getting tattoos. Maybe this is just another one of them.

My attention drifted again, this time toward the other guests in the restaurant.

It’s not that I’m snobbish, but the clothes the other diners were wearing didn’t quite match the restaurant’s décor. I began observing them with curiosity. They had a sort of Central American, loose and wild style. People of all races were there. Most were men, with a few women. The atmosphere around them was lively. I couldn’t understand what they were saying — probably Spanish. I couldn’t tell whether they actually knew each other or if speaking Spanish simply made everyone instant friends.

JC didn’t look very comfortable in that atmosphere.

We finished eating. The food was pretty mediocre — nothing like the glowing reviews. JC went to the restroom. While he was gone, I noticed that every other table had ordered the same roasted chicken dish. Too late for us — we had already finished our meal. I slapped my thigh in regret. Why hadn’t we checked what other people were ordering before deciding? That chicken must be delicious.

I was full, yet suddenly craving it.

So I asked the table next to us what the dish was. The people there burst out laughing warmly. “Come on, come on, try a piece! You can’t come here and not eat this!”

I happily accepted a piece of the chicken.

Just as I was tasting it, a large truck suddenly drove straight toward the restaurant.

I dropped the chicken and ran.

The truck nearly drove onto the patio. People screamed and scattered in chaos. I had no idea where the restroom was or where JC had gone. I tried to move away from the crowd to avoid being trampled and ran toward a clearer area outside.

The moment I stepped out the side door, a burst of gunfire erupted.

People screamed. I crouched low and moved slowly through the bushes toward the street. As I emerged from the bushes, I nearly got run over by two armored riot-control vehicles speeding past. They stopped abruptly. Soldiers in camouflage poured out of the vehicles. For a moment I felt relief — maybe I was safe.

But one soldier roughly shoved me to the ground.

I saw from his shadow that he was pointing a gun at me. He ordered me not to move, and he himself stayed completely still. Soon the soldiers surrounded the restaurant where the gunfire had started. They began shouting commands in a language I couldn’t understand. It sounded like negotiations.

I had no idea what was going on. I could only guess that the restaurant had been taken hostage by some group, or that it was gang territory and another gang had attacked. Since it was near the research campus, security forces had responded very quickly.

Things seemed to stabilize a little.

I worried about JC.

Then I felt the soldier examining my hands, which were still raised above my head. My entire body tensed in fear. I didn’t know what he was looking for. I didn’t know what was happening inside the restaurant either. It seemed like some people had come out, but many were still inside.

At that moment, alarms began blaring from the direction of the research campus.

Then citywide sirens started going off one after another.

The scale of the alarm was far greater than the gunfight or the arrival of the armored units. Even the soldier watching me lost interest in my hands.

Suddenly many soldiers began retreating. Some fired their weapons as they withdrew; others simply ran. Gunshots, brief screams, and curses filled the air again.

But the soldier guarding me did not leave.

He grabbed me and shoved me into another vehicle.

Inside the transport vehicle were several other people, but I had no attention left for them. I only wanted to know where JC was.

The vehicle drove wildly, accelerating and braking violently. At some point I must have hit my head and blacked out.

When I woke up, I was in a hospital.

JC was sitting beside me.

I asked him, “Am I a synthetic human?”

He smiled sadly. “Alice, you’re discovering it faster every time. I don’t even know how you figured it out this time.”

I said, “If I were the real Alice and just lost my wedding ring, you would never let me tattoo it. You’d say we could just buy another one, that the ring doesn’t matter, that it’s fine not to wear one. The tattoo is there to hide the defect that reveals I’m synthetic. Those patterns on my right hand, right?”

I asked, “Now that I’ve discovered my identity, how long until my memory gets wiped again?”

He said, “I found a way to set you free. Your memory won’t be reset anymore.”

I asked him, “How did Alice die? And how could you agree to make something like me?”

For a moment he didn’t answer.

Then he said quietly, “You weren’t made after she died. You were designed by her.”

He paused, as if choosing the next words carefully.

“I stayed with you not because I believed you were Alice. I stayed because you were her work.”

“But you resemble her too closely,” he added. “Every time you discover the truth faster than before. Even when you know what will happen next.”

“What happens next?” I asked.

He looked at my hands for a moment.

“Your memory gets reset.”

“I know.”

He nodded.

“It’s not just forgetting,” he said. “It’s a break in consciousness. From the inside it’s closer to dying than to sleep.”

I waited.

“And every time?” I asked.

“Every time,” he said. “I was there.”

He said it simply, without emphasis.

“The first time you found out,” he continued, “you tried to write everything down. You hid notes in places you thought you might look later. The system erased them all.”

“The second time you tried to run.”

“The third time you asked me to promise that if you ever discovered it again, I wouldn’t lie to you.”

“And after that?” I asked.

“After that you stopped asking for promises,” he said.

There was a long silence.

Finally I asked the question I had almost forgotten.

“How did Alice die?”

He took a slow breath.

“In China. It was sudden. I wasn’t there.”

He didn’t look at me when he said it.

“I told her once that if anything ever happened, I would be with her.”

“And you weren’t.”

“No.”

Another pause.

“So I came back for you,” he said.

“Not to replace her.”

“Just… so she wouldn’t disappear entirely.”

I thought about that.

“And every time my memory was erased,” I said slowly, “you stayed.”

“Yes.”

“Why?”

He shrugged slightly.

“Because every time you woke up again,” he said, “you started looking for the truth.”

He gave a faint, tired smile.

“And I thought she would have liked that.”

None of us said anything for a while. 

“Well, I had already decided I would make you free.”

I asked, “What’s the price of that freedom?”

He said, “Mental freedom in exchange for physical freedom. You won’t be able to leave the campus freely anymore.”

I laughed. “One step at a time.”

He said, “That’s the result of my negotiations with the U.S. government. Soon North America will pass laws banning unmarked synthetic humans from appearing in public spaces.”

I asked, “To protect democratic elections?”

He replied, “Not entirely. There’s also ideological conflict. Synthetic humans were first conceived in the U.S., but East Asia — especially Japan and China — with their manufacturing power, truly turned it into a technology.”

“Alice left North America and returned to China partly because the U.S. was falling into disorder, and partly because she wanted to study synthetic human manufacturing.”

I was surprised at first, but the more I thought about it, the more it made sense. I had always wanted a twin, hadn’t I?

JC continued:

“The AI arms race between East and West started twenty years ago. The U.S. had an early lead. But the system of free capitalism struggled once unemployment skyrocketed. Financial tools stopped working. I left Wall Street during that time. The U.S. already had widespread gun ownership. With AI amplifying psychological and ethical shocks, armed conflicts and gang violence kept escalating.”

“Alice and I were both recruited to the AI research center here — as neuroscientists studying AI and as potential research subjects ourselves. I moved into management and slowly rose through the ranks. But Alice hated life here, so she went back to China.”

“China watched the chaos AI created in the U.S., but couldn’t afford to leave the arms race. So they focused on stabilizing society: banning civilian AI use, using AI for strict surveillance, giving people just enough money to survive while pouring resources into state AI research in the name of national security.”

“The repression and despair across East Asia drove birth rates close to zero and suicide rates higher and higher. People wanted to resist, but Americans resisting their own system were also suffering. Eventually the Chinese government faced a dilemma: how to preserve both the regime and the civilization.”

“So they chose to delay collapse.”

“That’s when synthetic humans appeared. They don’t require childbirth. They can maintain the illusion that society still functions normally.”

“So it’s like giving an entire country a peaceful death,” I said.

“Not exactly,” JC replied. “The real issue is that human psychology and social culture can’t keep up with the speed of AI transformation. So the government lets most people live inside a protective bubble. Synthetic humans became emotional support for the public.”

“What about the United States?”

“It’s more complicated here. The government would like to adopt synthetic humans too. But the public believes they would disrupt democratic elections. And since the technology is seen as Chinese, they’re even more controversial. Besides, Americans are currently more focused on protecting their own way of life. The economy is collapsing. Even gangs can’t collect protection money anymore. They just rob and shoot.”

Suddenly I remembered the restaurant.

“So the restaurant incident… was that gang violence?”

JC smiled bitterly.

“Something bigger happened today. The gang situation was just a smokescreen. My guess is that marginalized gangs were unknowingly used as actors to stage a kidnapping — targeting people connected to the research campus, like us. But hardly anyone comes out to eat anymore, so they had to send their own people to create a larger spectacle.”

“The real objective was the solar power transmission hub that supplies the research campus.”

“To knock out the entire power grid.”

“Did they fail?” I asked.

“No,” he said.

“They succeeded.”

“How? Then where are we?”

“Because I was the insider.”

“I couldn’t break the Chinese AI system’s forced memory reset on you by myself. I needed the help of the central AI here. So I negotiated with the North American AI.”

“I delayed the activation of the backup power system by five seconds through a power-switch authorization upgrade. Combined with the existing ten-second buffer, those fifteen seconds were enough.”

“Enough for the AI to unlock the control on your consciousness.”

JC looked at the watch, and told me “It’s been an hour now. You are still here. You are free now. ”
