Before I noticed my long-unseen friend, I first saw a fresh touch of purple among the crowded supermarket aisles and shelves. It was a little girl sitting in a shopping cart, wearing a princess tulle dress. Her hair was braided, secured with two purple hair clip holding back loose strands. Looking closer, she even had purple-and-white striped leggings on—an outfit carefully coordinated and striking.

Then I saw the side profile of the person pushing the cart. It was my friend Z.

I walked toward her and greeted her. She turned around, smiling brightly. Yet within that cheerful expression, there was a fatigue that had been there for a long time, and something like a lingering trace of sadness.

I remembered the last time we had been alone together. She had told me that after having a child, she no longer had any time of her own. She also didn’t want others—especially her mother-in-law—to be too involved in raising the child. I once suggested she encourage her husband to take care of their daughter more. She had laughed and said the little girl only wanted her, not her dad.

This time, I had assumed they were grocery shopping as a couple. Instead, it was just her, bringing her three-year-old on the SkyTrain to buy groceries. I felt surprised, and also a mixture of tenderness and quiet dissatisfaction.

I walked over happily and complimented the little girl on her dress. The child looked me up and down, covered the lower half of her face with her hand, tilted her head shyly to the side. My friend encouraged her to talk to me and tell me her name. She remained silent, just smiling. I didn’t mind at all and quickly said not to pressure her.

I complimented my friend on dressing her so thoughtfully. She laughed.
“Not at all—it’s all her choice now. Half the clothes I buy, she refuses to wear. She loves dressing up. Today she insisted on this outfit. It’s really a spring-summer dress, but she wouldn’t leave the house without it.”

I praised the child’s sense of style and fashion potential, then joked about my own mediocre taste in clothes. Unexpectedly, my friend joined in, saying she never understood fashion either—growing up, she just wore whatever her mother bought for her. Looking at her more closely—burgundy Roots sweatshirt and jeans, nothing remarkable—I thought the child really did care more about clothing than her mother.

She told me the little girl had recently started daycare, her mother-in-law had gone back to her home country, and now she had to cook on her own again. As we walked, half of our conversation revolved around what the child liked.

From time to time, the girl tugged at her clothes and called “Mama,” seeking attention. My friend never hesitated to pause our conversation and respond to her needs.

The child wanted candy. They negotiated briefly and the girl got two M&Ms.

“She likes M&Ms, but we can’t give her too many—it’s not good for brain and eye development.”

For someone without children like me, this was completely new knowledge. But my friend, an engineering graduate, had clearly done her research in child nutrition.

We passed the yogurt section. She noticed a pouch of fruit purée I had never paid attention to—something like a squeezable jelly—and handed it to the girl, who hugged it happily.

At the meat counter, she commented that the veal ribs didn’t look great today.
I said, “Veal ribs are a bit fatty. I’m controlling my weight these days, so I don’t eat them much.”
She replied that her daughter liked them.
I laughed. “Well, she’s growing—she can eat freely.”

We exchanged quick recipes for convenient, family-friendly meals.

Then the fruit pouch dropped onto the floor. She quickly picked it up and handed it back to the child.

We moved on to the prepared foods section. She said the pasta her daughter liked wasn’t there—maybe not stocked yet, or already sold out. I recommended a tuna-cranberry-onion salad I liked, though I wasn’t sure if children could eat it. Her eyes really lit up.

“That sounds good. I like tuna! Where is it?”

We each took a box and returned to the cart. Then I heard the sound again—the pouch had fallen once more. We both bent down; she was faster. She tried putting it in the cart, but the child wanted it back. With a trace of impatience, she said, “If you want it, hold it properly. If you can’t, then put it in the cart.”

Only then did I realize that this time it hadn’t slipped through the cart’s gaps—the girl had dropped it from her hands. My friend explained, “She’s little. She can’t hold things well.”

We continued walking.

In the bakery section, I excitedly introduced sourdough bread—slow glucose release, good for insulin response, though the sourness can be an acquired taste. I suggested splitting one since it was too big for me alone. I demonstrated the automatic bread-cutting machine, telling them it was an “advanced skill,” laughing as I operated it "as long as you have hands". The little girl watched intently.

We divided the bread, and I insisted on paying. My friend tried to reimburse me for her half, but I waved it off. “It’s only a couple dollars. You’re helping me avoid waste.”

As we headed toward the desserts, I heard that familiar light thud again. The pouch had fallen. This time her tone was sharper.

“You insist on holding it, but you can’t hold it properly. Why not just put it in the cart? How many times has it fallen already?”

I turned slightly away, giving the two of them space.

After checkout, we put the groceries in my car and planned to walk about 160 meters to a specialty mushroom shop. They had a small greenhouse where children could see how mushrooms grow.

Outside the supermarket, she firmly wanted the girl to wear a down vest—it was only about 10°C. The child refused; her love for beauty outweighed the cold. My friend tried to negotiate.

“If you put on the vest, Mama will carry you.”

I thought the child would agree. She didn’t. She refused the vest and just insisted on being carried.

My friend lost patience. She stopped speaking, put the vest on her anyway, and lifted her quickly.

I asked how much she weighed now. “Almost thirty pounds,” she said.

“That’s heavy to carry all the time,” I said. “The store is close. Let’s walk slowly.”

She tried setting her down. After a few steps, the child demanded to be carried again. She took a few more steps, then bent down and lifted her.

She laughed at herself.
“Before I had a child, I used to think carrying a kid was so heavy. Now I understand. My strength has grown with her weight. She didn’t become this heavy in one day.”

After buying mushrooms, we returned to my car and waited for her husband to pick them up. I suggested several times that I could just drive them home—it was on my way—but she insisted on waiting.

He arrived later than expected. The child was hungry and received four more M&Ms.

I noticed her place the last M&M on her right palm and press her left index finger on it, saying, “Daddy, come quickly.”

I asked jokingly, “Is she casting a spell to summon him?”

My friend laughed. “She thinks if you keep pressing the elevator button, it comes faster. So she thinks if she presses the M&M, Daddy will come faster.”

I smiled at the childish logic. I suddenly remembered my own childhood. When my parents scolded me angrily, I would go to the bathroom and shrink the space their towels occupied on the rack, expanding mine instead—trying to balance our ‘energy fields.’

My childhood was not always warm and gentle. I often felt small and controlled. As children, we don’t understand our parents’ judgments or demands, and we aren’t capable of bearing consequences. But this little girl—strong-willed and opinionated—must also face moments of helplessness and confusion. She must also want to challenge the authority and power adults already possess.

Watching her press that powerless M&M, trying to summon her father, I suddenly wondered:

Was the fruit pouch really falling because she couldn’t hold it?
