He said chimpanzees can tear apart and kill the infants of their own kind with the strength it takes to rip a basketball in half, without even blinking. Bonobos, on the other hand, seem to spend their entire lives making love rather than making war.

# Research Journal

**May 1, 2057**

Today is worth celebrating.

The proposal for the **first phase of the project** has finally been approved!

The ethics committee was unbelievably picky. I wasted an entire month revising the proposal eight times.

Only after confirming that the artificial wombs contained **no human tissue**, and that the robots caring for the future infants contained **no technology from hostile nations**, did they finally sign off.

But the hardest part was convincing the foundation board to fund the project.

At first they said “interesting” with their mouths, but their eyes clearly said “boring,” and their minds were probably thinking about what to have for dinner.

Then my brilliant student suggested something:

If the project ultimately succeeded in creating **an ape society that approximates the peacefulness of human society**, we could conduct **social experiments on them**.

The moment they heard “improving humanity’s peace index” without experimenting on actual humans, the foundation suddenly became much more generous with their checks.

Ever since five years ago, when **World War III almost broke out**, and “fortunately” a sudden wave of extreme weather disrupted several eager nations, the shadow of war has pushed social psychology into unprecedented prominence.

Even the developmental psychology professor next door, who spent half his life studying childhood development, has turned his research toward figuring out what kind of school education could “reduce social fragmentation.”

After all these years they haven’t even made progress on healing childhood trauma theories, and now they think education alone can repair a divided society?

Please.

Different ethnic and political groups hardly attend the same schools anymore.

At today’s seminar, our collaborating animal psychology team presented the **first version of a humanoid-ape upbringing plan**.
