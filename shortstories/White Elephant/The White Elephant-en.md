**The White Elephant**

In the dream I found myself somewhere among crowds that looked Southeast Asian, or perhaps Indian. In the dream I believed I was in Thailand, though in truth I had no idea where it really was.

Men and women in loose, brightly colored clothes crowded along the roadside, staring toward the middle of the road. Many of the women wore large golden necklaces, bracelets, and anklets with strong ethnic styles. Everyone was barefoot, standing on soft, damp earth.

I couldn’t see anything, so I tried to push forward through the crowd. I asked someone what they were watching.

An old man told me they were choosing a princess.

I said, “Why do they need to choose a princess? Doesn’t the king have daughters?”

He replied that this princess was not a royal heir, but a sacred maiden chosen for religious reasons — a national treasure chosen by a pure white elephant.

I asked him how the elephant chose.

“Just keep watching,” he said.

Soon I saw it: an enormous white elephant slowly walking into view.

A little girl in traditional dress stepped forward. She had a long thin braid tied behind her head and a band across her forehead. She held a short stick with both hands, gripping each end.

The elephant curled its trunk around the middle of the stick.

Then suddenly the elephant swung its trunk violently.

The poor girl was flung high and far through the air. I had no idea where she landed.

The old man said calmly, “Whoever doesn’t get thrown away, killed, or crippled by the elephant becomes the princess.”

In the dream I somehow accepted this strange rule without question.

Then the old man asked if I wanted to try.

I said, “There’s no need. I don’t want to be a princess.”

He began to say, “But if you become the princess, you will—”

Before he could finish, the white elephant started walking straight toward me. The crowd scattered instantly.

When I turned my head, even the old man had disappeared somewhere in the rushing crowd.

The elephant came very close. I stepped back. It blew a puff of warm air at me and then shoved me hard with its trunk.

I got annoyed.

So I grabbed the elephant’s trunk.

Suddenly everything spun. The world seemed to shake as the elephant swung its trunk wildly, and I held on with all my strength.

Eventually it stopped. The elephant lifted its trunk high into the air, and I was hanging from it.

When I squinted down, I realized I must have been several meters above the ground. That made me even more afraid to let go.

The dream fades out there — or perhaps I simply forgot the rest.

When it continues again, I am already sitting on the elephant’s back.

Now the elephant was no longer bare. A red cloth embroidered with golden threads covered its head, though I could only see part of it. I was sitting inside something like a small carriage mounted on its back, on a chair with thin gauze-like curtains hanging on both sides.

The elephant walked slowly forward, swaying as it moved.

People on both sides cheered. I was much higher than everyone else, and it felt strange to see the crowd from that perspective for the first time.

Eventually the elephant brought me to a white domed building.

The road leading to it was covered with red carpets. Outside the carpets were flowers. Beyond the flowers were crowds of people.

Just as I slid down from the elephant’s back and began walking toward the palace, I woke up.

I had this dream when I was eleven years old.

A year or two later, when I first saw a picture of the **Taj Mahal** in a book, it strangely felt familiar.
